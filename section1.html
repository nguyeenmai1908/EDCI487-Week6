One Planet's Worth of Year
~5 minutes

Imagine you earn $50,000 a year, but spend $90,000. To make up the difference, you draw down savings, run up credit cards, and start selling things you own — the car, the bike, your grandmother's ring.

For a while, it works. The lights stay on. The fridge stays full. You feel, on most days, like you're doing fine.

But the math doesn't change. Your spending is bigger than your earning. The savings are finite. Eventually, the gap closes — either because you choose to spend less, or because there's nothing left to sell.

Most of us know what it feels like to spend more than we earn. That's not metaphorical knowledge — it's lived. The overshoot framing borrows that lived knowledge and applies it to nature.

But the metaphor only gets us partway. A household budget is one person's numbers. A planetary budget is shared — and the people doing most of the spending are often not the same people footing the bill.

The concept climate education keeps skipping

Wackernagel and Lin ran a media search for the year 2022. Here's what they found:

  • Articles mentioning 'ecological overshoot': 476
  • Articles mentioning 'climate change': 6,430,000

Climate change appeared in over 13,000 times as many articles as overshoot. But climate change, in the framing of this week's reading, is 'merely one symptom of overshoot' — not the root condition.

If a doctor talked about a fever 13,000 times as much as the infection causing it, we'd notice. In public discussion of the environment, we don't.

That gap is why this week exists.

Source: Wackernagel & Lin (2023), p. 626.

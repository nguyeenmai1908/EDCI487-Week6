The Math of Demand and Regeneration
~10 minutes

Picture a forest. Each year, the trees grow some amount of new wood, the soil regenerates some amount of nutrients, the streams move some amount of water through. That is the forest's annual regeneration — what it can give back without losing itself.

Now picture cutting a little more wood than the forest grows each year. For one year, you would barely notice. For ten, the forest thins. For a hundred, it is gone — not because anyone "killed" it in a single act, but because demand exceeded regeneration, year after year, until the accumulated stock was used up.

That gap — between demand and regeneration — is overshoot.

Overshoot
"Overshoot is the state in which human demand, within a given time period, exceeds the amount ecosystems regenerate in the same time period. Overshoot is possible because the accumulated stock of ecosystems can be depleted. The accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere is one of the most prominent symptoms of overshoot. But global depletion is not a long-term option. Therefore, global overshoot will inevitably end."
Wackernagel, M., & Lin, D. (2023). Overshoot. In Handbook of the Anthropocene. Springer.

That definition rests on three moving parts. Here's how they fit together:

01
Demand
A measurable quantity: the resources and waste-absorption capacity humanity uses each year. Calculated as the Ecological Footprint (in global hectares).
02
Regeneration
A measurable quantity: how much Earth's ecosystems renew each year — forests regrowing, fisheries replenishing, the atmosphere and oceans absorbing CO2. Calculated as biocapacity (in global hectares).
03
The Compounding
When demand exceeds regeneration, the gap doesn't just persist — it compounds. Each year's deficit draws down stock that took decades to build. And because the signals of damage (warming, species loss, soil depletion) arrive years or decades later, the overshoot grows long before anyone feels it.
The household-budget hook in Section 1 was not just a teaching device. Wackernagel and Lin use exactly this frame in the chapter:
"The relationship between deficit spending (annual emissions) and the accumulated debt (greenhouse gas concentration in the atmosphere), [runs] somewhat parallel to financial deficit and debt. As with finances, low levels of ecological debt are tolerable, but as ecological deficit spending continues, the debt becomes so large that its impact becomes difficult to manage."
— Wackernagel & Lin (2023), p. 627
The concrete example: adding 5 ppm of CO2 when the concentration is 300 ppm barely registers. Adding 5 ppm at today's concentration radically increases the likelihood of runaway climate change.

The average Canadian uses about five times more nature than the global biocapacity per person.

Notice what the country comparison reveals: overshoot is not a "humanity" problem in the simple sense. There is a humanity that lives within Earth's budget, and a humanity that does not. Wealth, geography, and consumption patterns matter.

This is where the system math meets the relational question. Demand is not anonymous. It has a return address.

The Histories That Got Left Out
~20 minutes

When you picture "an environmentalist," what comes to mind?

If your image is a white man in a Patagonia jacket on a Sierra Club hike — or John Muir, or Teddy Roosevelt, or Rachel Carson — that's not your fault. It's the version of environmental history most North American education has taught for the past sixty years. It's also incomplete.

The next 13 minutes give you a different version of that history. Watch it before you read on.

Watch for
01 Names you've never heard. Who? And what did they do?
02 Movements you've never heard of. When did they happen, and where?

Title: The Intersectional History of Environmentalism Creator: Intersectional Environmentalist Length: ~13 minutes

Three things to hold in mind

These are for your own thinking — not submitted. Let them sit with you and bring your responses to class.

01

Name one figure or movement from the video that you'd never been taught before. Why do you think their work was left out of the mainstream environmental story?

Need a starting point?
Consider race, class, geography, time period, and what counts as a "leader" in the dominant version.
02

The video traces a pattern: mainstream environmentalism often centered conservation of "wilderness" while sidelining the communities (often Black, Indigenous, low-income) most exposed to pollution and environmental harm. Where do you see this pattern still playing out today?

Need a starting point?
Think about climate marches you've seen, environmental orgs you've heard of, who gets quoted as an "expert" in climate news, whose neighbourhoods get the new park vs. the new highway.
03

Connect this back to Section 2. Overshoot is driven disproportionately by wealthy nations and wealthy consumers. The communities most harmed by overshoot's symptoms — extreme heat, food insecurity, displacement — are often the ones who contributed least to causing it. What does that connection ask of climate education?

Need a starting point?
"Asks of" can mean: what to teach, who to centre, what to question, what to refuse.
Key terms
Intersectionality (Kimberle Crenshaw, 1989)

A framework for understanding how different aspects of identity — race, gender, class, sexuality, disability, etc. — overlap to shape a person's experience of power and oppression. Originally developed to describe how Black women experienced forms of discrimination that single-axis analyses (race-only OR gender-only) couldn't see.

Intersectional Environmentalism (Leah Thomas, 2020)

An inclusive version of environmentalism that advocates for the protection of both people and the planet. It names how environmental harm and social injustice are interlinked — and centres the communities most affected.

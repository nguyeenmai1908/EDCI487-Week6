## SECTION 1 — HOOK: ONE PLANET'S WORTH OF YEAR (`section1.html`)

**Section header:**
- Section 1 of 5
- Title: **One Planet's Worth of Year**
- Time: ~5 minutes

### Opening paragraphs (cream)

> Imagine you earn $50,000 a year, but spend $90,000. To make up the difference, you draw down savings, run up credit cards, and start selling things you own — the car, the bike, your grandmother's ring.
>
> For a while, it works. The lights stay on. The fridge stays full. You feel, on most days, like you're doing fine.
>
> But the math doesn't change. Your spending is bigger than your earning. The savings are finite. Eventually, the gap closes — either because you choose to spend less, or because there's nothing left to sell.

### Reveal block (clay accent, large number)

**Pre-headline:** Earth Overshoot Day 2025
**Big stat:** July 24
**Sub:** The date by which humanity used up everything Earth can regenerate in 2025.

**Body (small):**
> From July 24 onward, every resource humanity used — every fish caught, tree cut, kilo of CO₂ emitted beyond what forests and oceans could absorb — was drawn from accumulated stock. Not income. Capital.

### Why this metaphor matters (block, italic body text)

> Most of us know what it feels like to spend more than we earn. That's not metaphorical knowledge — it's lived. The overshoot framing borrows that lived knowledge and applies it to nature.
>
> But the metaphor only gets us partway. A household budget is *one person's* numbers. A planetary budget is shared — and the people doing most of the spending are often not the same people footing the bill.

## SECTION 2 — CONCEPT: WHAT OVERSHOOT IS (`section2.html`)

**Section header:**
- Section 2 of 5
- Title: **The Math of Demand and Regeneration**
- Time: ~10 minutes

### Opening (example-first, before the definition)

> Picture a forest. Each year, the trees grow some amount of new wood, the soil regenerates some amount of nutrients, the streams move some amount of water through. That's the forest's annual *regeneration* — what it can give back without losing itself.
>
> Now picture cutting a little more wood than the forest grows each year. For one year, you'd barely notice. For ten, the forest thins. For a hundred, it's gone — not because anyone "killed" it in a single act, but because demand exceeded regeneration, year after year, until the accumulated stock was used up.
>
> That gap — between demand and regeneration — is overshoot.

### Definition card (sage/cream box with attribution)

**Heading:** Overshoot
**Definition:**
> Overshoot is the state in which human demand, within a given time period, exceeds the amount ecosystems regenerate in the same time period. Overshoot is possible because the accumulated stock of ecosystems can be depleted. The accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere is one of the most prominent symptoms of overshoot. But global depletion is not a long-term option. Therefore, global overshoot will inevitably end.

**Attribution:** Wackernagel, M., & Lin, D. (2023). *Overshoot.* In *Handbook of the Anthropocene.* Springer.

### Three-part breakdown (concept-grid, cream cards)

**01 · Demand**
A measurable quantity: the resources and waste-absorption capacity humanity uses each year. Calculated as the *Ecological Footprint* (in global hectares).

**02 · Regeneration**
A measurable quantity: how much Earth's ecosystems renew each year — forests regrowing, fisheries replenishing, the atmosphere and oceans absorbing CO₂. Calculated as *biocapacity* (in global hectares).

**03 · The overshoot**
The gap between demand and regeneration. When demand > regeneration, the difference is taken from accumulated stock. The most visible symptom is CO₂ in the atmosphere — what forests and oceans cannot absorb stays.

### The deficit-and-debt frame (sage callout, source-attributed)

> The household-budget hook in Section 1 wasn't just a teaching device. Wackernagel and Lin use exactly this frame in the chapter:
>
> *"The relationship between deficit spending (annual emissions) and the accumulated debt (greenhouse gas concentration in the atmosphere), [runs] somewhat parallel to financial deficit and debt. As with finances, low levels of ecological debt are tolerable, but as ecological deficit spending continues, the debt becomes so large that its impact becomes difficult to manage."*
>
> — Wackernagel & Lin (2023), p. 627
>
> The concrete example: adding 5 ppm of CO₂ when the concentration is 300 ppm barely registers. Adding 5 ppm at today's concentration *radically increases the likelihood of runaway climate change.*

### Why the overshoot can grow so large (cream, callback to Week 5)

> Remember the CAS property from Week 5 — *feedback loops*. Wackernagel and Lin explain that when feedbacks are **weak or time-delayed**, demand can massively exceed regeneration before the system pushes back. Climate change is the textbook case: emissions today produce warming decades later, so the "signal" arrives too late to trigger self-correction in time.
>
> When feedbacks are **strong and fast**, ecosystems oscillate — small overshoots get corrected. When feedbacks are **slow or muted**, small overshoots compound into large ones, and correction only comes through crash rather than adjustment.
>
> This is why overshoot behaves the way it does: not because humans are uniquely destructive, but because the feedbacks we rely on to notice damage are structurally slow.

### The relational pivot (clay block, slightly larger text)

> Notice what the country comparison reveals: **overshoot is not a "humanity" problem in the simple sense**. There is a humanity that lives within Earth's budget, and a humanity that doesn't. Wealth, geography, and consumption patterns matter.
>
> This is where the system math meets the relational question. *Demand* is not anonymous. It has a return address.

### Definitions footer (cream block, two small definitions for vocabulary the video uses)

**Intersectionality** *(Kimberlé Crenshaw, 1989)*
A framework for understanding how different aspects of identity — race, gender, class, sexuality, disability, etc. — overlap to shape a person's experience of power and oppression. Originally developed to describe how Black women experienced forms of discrimination that single-axis analyses (race-only OR gender-only) couldn't see.

**Intersectional Environmentalism** *(Leah Thomas, 2020)*
An inclusive version of environmentalism that advocates for the protection of both people and the planet. It names how environmental harm and social injustice are interlinked — and centres the communities most affected.

## SECTION 4 — CONNECT: RELATIONAL THINKING ACROSS THE COURSE (`section4.html`)

**Section header:**
- Section 4 of 5
- Title: **Who's in the "We"?**
- Time: ~7 minutes

### Opening (cream)

> Climate education has a habit of using "we" in a way that flattens the conversation.
>
> *"We* need to reduce our emissions." *"We* have to consume less." *"We* are running out of time."
>
> The relational lens asks a sharper question: *which we?* The 1.8-Earth average is a global mean. The actual demand sits unevenly across nations, classes, industries, and individual households. The actual harm sits even more unevenly. And the actual knowledge for responding — including ways of relating to land that don't generate overshoot in the first place — often sits with the people the mainstream movement has historically ignored.

### Connection grid (cream, four cards showing the throughline across the course)

**Heading:** What we've built so far

**Week 3 · Climate Justice**
You learned that climate impacts fall hardest on marginalized communities. *That's the harm side of the relational equation.*

**Week 4 · Carbon Colonialism**
You learned that "solutions" like carbon offsets can shift the cost of consumption from rich nations to the lands and labour of poorer ones. *That's the response side, sometimes badly done.*

**Week 5 · Planetary Boundaries**
You learned that the Earth system has hard limits, and seven of nine have been crossed. Wackernagel and Lin add a piece we didn't yet have language for: *"Planetary boundaries are closely related to overshoot, but measure components of it, not the whole."* Planetary boundaries name the thresholds; overshoot names the underlying dynamic. *That's the system side — the math.*

**Week 6 · Relational Perspectives & Overshoot**
You're learning that the demand crossing those boundaries is unevenly distributed, that the history of environmentalism has erased the voices most needed for a just response, and that overshoot will end — by design or disaster. *That's the relational side — the who.*

### Relational thinking, defined (cream, light box)

> **Relational thinking** treats humans and non-human systems as continuously shaping each other. It refuses the separation between "the environment" (out there, in nature) and "society" (in here, in cities and laws).
>
> This is not just a philosophical stance. It's increasingly how Earth system science itself works — and it's how many Indigenous knowledge systems have always worked. Recognizing relation is the first move toward responding without reproducing harm.

## SECTION 5 — INTO YOUR CLASSROOM (`section5.html`)

**Section header:**
- Section 5 of 5
- Title: **Teaching the Relational Move**
- Time: ~5 minutes

### Opening (cream)

> You'll be teaching students who are already exposed to climate content — much of it framed in the language of individual responsibility ("turn off the lights," "use a reusable bottle"). Helpful, in their place. Insufficient, on their own.
>
> Relational climate education doesn't replace those messages. It widens them. It puts the individual lightbulb back inside the system that made the lightbulb necessary — and inside the histories that shaped who has access to which lightbulbs in the first place.

### Three teaching moves (cream, three cards with example questions)

**01 · Move from "what" to "who"**
> Instead of "*what* are the effects of climate change?" — try *"who* experiences these effects first, and *who* has the resources to adapt?"
>
> *Why it works:* It turns abstract impacts into questions about power, geography, and history — at any grade level.

**02 · Connect scale without flattening it**
> Pair a local observation ("our school's heat days have gone up") with a global pattern ("Earth Overshoot Day has moved earlier every decade") — but always ask: *who in our community is most affected by our heat days, and who isn't?*
>
> *Why it works:* It builds the muscle of seeing systems without losing the people inside them.

**03 · Centre voices the curriculum has marginalized**
> When teaching climate, include sources, leaders, and knowledge systems that the dominant story has left out. Robert Bullard. Hazel Johnson. Indigenous land defenders in your region. Leah Thomas. The Lək̓ʷəŋən and WSÁNEĆ peoples on whose territories your future classroom likely stands.
>
> *Why it works:* It models, for your students, that knowledge is never neutral about whose it is.

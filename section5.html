Teaching the Relational Move
~5 minutes

You'll be teaching students who are already exposed to climate content — much of it framed in the language of individual responsibility ('turn off the lights,' 'use a reusable bottle'). Helpful, in their place. Insufficient, on their own.

Relational climate education doesn't replace those messages. It widens them. It puts the individual lightbulb back inside the system that made the lightbulb necessary — and inside the histories that shaped who has access to which lightbulbs in the first place.

01

Move from "what" to "who"

Instead of 'what are the effects of climate change?' — try 'who experiences these effects first, and who has the resources to adapt?'

Why it works: It turns abstract impacts into questions about power, geography, and history — at any grade level.

02

Connect scale without flattening it

Pair a local observation ('our school's heat days have gone up') with a global pattern ('Earth Overshoot Day has moved earlier every decade') — but always ask: who in our community is most affected by our heat days, and who isn't?

Why it works: It builds the muscle of seeing systems without losing the people inside them.

03

Centre voices the curriculum has marginalized

When teaching climate, include sources, leaders, and knowledge systems that the dominant story has left out. Robert Bullard. Hazel Johnson. Indigenous land defenders in your region. Leah Thomas. The Lek̀wəŋən and WSÁNEĆ peoples on whose territories your future classroom likely stands.

Why it works: It models, for your students, that knowledge is never neutral about whose it is.

By Design, Not by Disaster

You've spent this week with one of the most uncomfortable facts in climate science: that overshoot, by definition, ends. And you've spent it with one of the most uncomfortable facts in environmental history: that the people who knew most about living without overshoot have often been the least listened to.

Both facts ask the same thing of you as a future teacher: to widen the 'we' — in your curriculum, in your sources, in the questions you let your students ask.

What you've engaged with this week
01

Overshoot: Human demand on nature exceeds what ecosystems regenerate each year — currently by a factor of 1.8. The state is not viable long-term. It ends, the only question is how.

02

Earth Overshoot Day 2025 fell on July 24. From that point, every resource used was drawn from accumulated stock, not annual income. Country-level overshoot days reveal that demand sits very unevenly across the world.

03

Intersectional environmentalism names how environmental harm and social injustice are interlinked. The mainstream environmental movement has historically erased the voices of Black, Indigenous, and low-income communities most exposed to environmental damage.

04

Relational thinking treats human and non-human systems as continuously shaping each other. It refuses the separation between "environment" and "society" and connects directly to long-standing Indigenous worldviews.

05

Teaching relationally means moving from what to who, connecting local to global without flattening, and centring the voices a standard curriculum leaves out.

Who's in the "We"?
~7 minutes

Climate education has a habit of using "we" in a way that flattens the conversation.

"We need to reduce our emissions." "We have to consume less." "We are running out of time."

The relational lens asks a sharper question: which we? The 1.8-Earth average is a global mean. The actual demand sits unevenly across nations, classes, industries, and individual households. The actual harm sits even more unevenly. And the actual knowledge for responding — including ways of relating to land that don't generate overshoot in the first place — often sits with the people the mainstream movement has historically ignored.

What we've built so far

Week 3 Climate Justice You learned that climate impacts fall hardest on marginalized communities. That's the harm side of the relational equation. That's the harm side.
Week 4 Carbon Colonialism You learned that "solutions" like carbon offsets can shift the cost of consumption from rich nations to the lands and labour of poorer ones. That's the response side — sometimes badly done.
Week 5 Planetary Boundaries You learned that the Earth system has hard limits, and seven of nine have been crossed. Wackernagel and Lin add a piece we didn't yet have language for: "Planetary boundaries are closely related to overshoot, but measure components of it, not the whole." Planetary boundaries name the thresholds; overshoot names the underlying dynamic. That's the system side — the math.
Week 6 Relational Perspectives & Overshoot You're learning that the demand crossing those boundaries is unevenly distributed, that the history of environmentalism has erased the voices most needed for a just response, and that overshoot will end — by design or disaster. That's the relational side — the who.

Relational thinking, defined

Relational thinking treats humans and non-human systems as continuously shaping each other. It refuses the separation between "the environment" (out there, in nature) and "society" (in here, in cities and laws).

This is not just a philosophical stance. It's increasingly how Earth system science itself works — and it's how many Indigenous knowledge systems have always worked. Recognizing relation is the first move toward responding without reproducing harm.

That last point deserves a pause — because it shapes everything that follows.

One thing to bring to class

With all of that in mind — the system math, the human history, the relational frame, and the question of whose knowledge counts — pick the one that catches you. Sit with it and bring your thinking to class.

  • "Whose voices have shaped your image of 'the environment' so far in your life?"
  • "Which Week 3-6 idea would change most about how you'd teach climate to a Grade 5 class — and how?"
  • "Wackernagel says overshoot will end 'by design or by disaster.' What does 'by design' look like, in your subject area or future classroom?"
